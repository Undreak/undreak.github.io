The Problem

When you spill water on a table, it spreads. The physics seems straightforwardâ€”until you look closely. At the moving edge of a spreading droplet, classical fluid mechanics predicts infinite energy dissipation. This is the Huh-Scriven paradox, and resolving it requires understanding what happens at the nanoscale.

The solution involves a precursor film: a thin layer of liquid, just nanometers thick, that races ahead of the visible droplet edge. This film dissipates the energy that would otherwise blow up in our equations. On atomically smooth surfaces, we understand this well. But real surfaces are never atomically smooth.

Here's where it gets interesting. On surfaces with micrometer-scale roughness, a different phenomenon takes over: hemiwicking, where capillary forces drive liquid through surface features. The physics changes completely. But what happens in betweenâ€”when surface defects are nanometers tall, comparable to the precursor film itself?

Does the classical spreading law still hold when surface roughness matches the precursor film thickness? Does a precursor film even form under these conditions?

This was unknown. I set out to answer these questions experimentally.

Microscope image (top view) of a droplet spreading on a smooth surface, showing Newton's rings interference pattern

Why It's Hard

Three challenges made this problem experimentally inaccessible until now:

Fabrication: Creating surfaces with controlled nanoscale defects (20â€“80 nm tall) distributed uniformly over centimeter-scale areas. Electron beam lithography is too slow. Photolithography isn't precise enough. I needed a different approach.

Characterization: Measuring defect heights of tens of nanometers while also mapping their spatial distribution across large areas requires combining multiple techniques at different scales.

Dynamics: Tracking a spreading droplet whose radius follows a \(t^{1/10}\) power law means the interesting behavior spans hours to days. This demands automated analysis of hundreds of image sequences.

My Approach

1. Engineering the Surfaces

I used soft lithography with charged silica nanoparticles (~100 nm diameter). The particles repel each other electrostatically, producing uniform distributions without clustering. By controlling two parametersâ€”sintering temperature (1130â€“1190Â°C) and incubation time (0â€“50 min)â€”I tuned defect height and density independently.

This produced 32 distinct surfaces spanning:

The sintering process also transforms defect geometry: at lower temperatures, defects remain spherical caps; at higher temperatures, they flatten into Gaussian-like profiles. This matters for the physics.

3. Automated Optical Analysis

Tracking droplet spreading manually across hundreds of experiments wasn't feasible. I built a Python pipeline that:

  • Detects interference fringes (Newton's rings) in the spreading droplet
  • Extracts droplet radius from fringe positions
  • Measures precursor film width from intensity gradients
  • Calculates droplet volume from the reconstructed height profile

The interference pattern encodes the droplet's shape: each bright fringe corresponds to a height change of \(\lambda/2n \approx 174\) nm (for 488 nm light in silicone oil with \(n = 1.4\)).

Results

The Central Finding

Classical theory predicts that a droplet spreading on a flat surface follows Tanner's law:

\[ r \sim \left(\frac{\gamma V^3 t}{\eta}\right)^{1/10} \]

where \(r\) is radius, \(V\) is volume, \(\gamma\) is surface tension, \(\eta\) is viscosity, and \(t\) is time. The exponent 1/10 emerges from balancing capillary driving forces against viscous dissipationâ€”it's a signature of the underlying physics.

The expectation was that nanoscale defects might disrupt this. When defect height approaches film thickness, the liquid encounters obstacles comparable to its own depth. Theoretical work by Cormier et al. suggested that sufficiently thick films could shift the dynamics to a different regime (\(t^{1/2}\) scaling).

What I found: Tanner's law holds across all tested surfaces.

Defect heights from 20â€“80 nm, densities from 0â€“12 defects/Î¼mÂ², viscosities spanning three orders of magnitudeâ€”the data collapse onto a single master curve when properly rescaled:

\[ r \text{ vs. } t/\tau, \quad \text{where } \tau = \eta/(\gamma V^3) \]

This is significant. It means that nanoscale roughness, at least within these parameters, doesn't fundamentally alter the spreading mechanism. The classical framework remains valid.

Master curve of the radius of droplets on various nano-textured surfaces following the predicted \(t^{1/10}\) scaling

The Precursor Film

On smooth surfaces, no visible film forms ahead of the droplet. On my nano-textured surfaces, a distinct film appearsâ€”and its dynamics follow a different law:

\[ W_f \sim \sqrt{D_1 t}, \quad \text{where } D_1 = \frac{2\gamma(r-1)h}{\eta} \]

Here \(W_f\) is film width, \(h\) is film height, and \(r\) is the roughness parameter from AFM measurements. This \(\sqrt{t}\) scaling is characteristic of diffusion-driven spreadingâ€”the hemiwicking regime.

The film is visible in optical microscopy as a gradient surrounding the droplet edge. Its width correlates with defect height: taller defects promote wider films. This makes physical senseâ€”larger surface features create stronger capillary driving forces.

An Unexpected Observation

For very small droplets (\(V < 0.001\) Î¼L), I observed something the theory doesn't predict: the macroscopic droplet disappears entirely while the film continues spreading. The droplet acts as a reservoir that the film drains completely.

This sets a practical lower bound on droplet volume for these experiments. It also raises questions about volume conservation assumptions in the theoretical framework.

Droplet disappearing after the film has depleted its reservoir

What This Means

For the Field

This is the first systematic experimental study of wetting dynamics on surfaces with controlled nanometric roughness. Previous work examined either atomically smooth surfaces (validating precursor films) or micrometer-scale textures (characterizing hemiwicking). The nanoscale regimeâ€”where defect height matches film thicknessâ€”remained unexplored.

The key result is that Tanner's law is more robust than expected. This constrains theoretical models: any framework for wetting on rough surfaces must reduce to \(t^{1/10}\) scaling in this parameter range.

For Applications

Understanding when classical wetting theory appliesâ€”and when it breaks downâ€”matters for:

Coating processes: If you're designing a coating that must spread uniformly over a textured substrate, knowing that Tanner's law holds at the nanoscale means you can use established models for process design. The texture won't introduce unexpected dynamics.

Microfluidics: Channel surfaces in microfluidic devices are rarely atomically smooth. This work suggests that moderate nanoscale roughness won't fundamentally alter capillary-driven flow, which simplifies device design.

Inkjet printing: Droplet spreading on paper or polymer substrates involves nanoscale surface features. The robustness of Tanner's law provides a predictive framework.

Open Questions

Several observations remain unexplained:

Where does Tanner's law break down? My parameter range (20â€“80 nm height, 0â€“12 defects/Î¼mÂ²) showed consistent \(t^{1/10}\) scaling. Larger defects or different geometries might reveal transitions to other regimes.

What determines film thickness? I observed films optically but couldn't measure their thickness directly. Neutron or X-ray reflectivity could resolve this.

Why do small droplets vanish? The critical volume below which the droplet is entirely consumed by the film, and the mechanism driving this transition, need quantification.

What causes batch-to-batch variation in oxide layers? Some 1190Â°C surfaces showed unexpected differences in film visibility despite identical protocols. The spreading dynamics were unaffected, but the origin of this variability is unclear.
