Wetting Dynamics on Nano-Textured Surfaces

A systematic study of how liquids spread on precisely engineered surfaces at the nanoscale

Master's Thesis June - September 2024 CEA Saclay

Duration: June - September 2024

Location: Laboratoire L√©on Brillouin, CEA Saclay

Field: Soft Matter Physics & Surface Science

The Big Picture

When you spill water on a table, it spreads. Simple, right? But what happens when that surface isn't smooth‚Äîwhen it has tiny bumps and valleys just nanometers tall? This is crucial for everything from waterproof coatings to lab-on-a-chip devices.

While wetting on rough surfaces has been studied for decades, precisely controlling nanoscale defects to study their specific effects remained challenging. This project aimed to answer: Do the classical laws of wetting still apply when surfaces have controlled nanometer-scale roughness?

Microscope image (top view) of a droplet spreading on a smooth surface

The Challenge

Traditional wetting theory assumes perfectly smooth surfaces. But real-world surfaces are never perfectly smooth‚Äîthey have defects, roughness, and texture. The question was:

Can we control nano-scale surface features precisely enough to study their effect on wetting dynamics?

The technical challenges:

  • Creating surfaces with controlled nanoscale defects (20-80 nm tall)
  • Measuring liquid spreading at micrometer scales in real-time
  • Analyzing hundreds of spreading events to get statistically meaningful results

My Approach

To answer this question, I needed to control three things: create surfaces with precisely defined nanoscale features, characterize them at multiple scales, and measure spreading dynamics across enough droplets for statistical significance. This required combining surface fabrication, microscopy, scattering techniques, and automated image analysis.

1. Engineering Nano-Textured Surfaces

I fabricated 32 different surfaces using silica nanoparticles. By controlling the sintering temperature (1130-1190¬∞C) and incubation time (0-50 minutes), I could precisely tune:

  • Defect height: 20-80 nanometers (about 1/1000th the width of a human hair)
  • Defect density: 0-12 defects per square micrometer

2. Characterization with Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM)

I characterized the surfaces using AFM, creating 3D maps of the nanoscale topography. This wasn't just about measuring roughness‚Äîit was about understanding exactly how the surface structure affects spreading.

3. Automated Analysis Pipeline

The analysis required processing >300 image sequences‚Äîeach one capturing how a droplet spreads over time. Manual analysis wasn't practical at this scale.

I built a Python pipeline that:

  • Automatically detects interference patterns in the droplet
  • Extracts droplet radius and film width over time
  • Fits the data to physical laws

Tools: NumPy, SciPy, scikit-image

Droplet image analysis showing interference patterns and extracted features

4. Multi-Scale Characterization

To validate the surface fabrication, I measured nanoparticle diameter using two complementary techniques:

  • SAXS (Small-Angle X-ray Scattering) using a XEUSS 2.0 spectrometer
  • SANS (Small-Angle Neutron Scattering) at ILL with the SAM instrument

Result: 106.2 ¬± 8.5 nm diameter, consistent with the manufacturer's specification.

Key Findings

Main Finding

The classical Tanner law still holds on nano-textured surfaces.

Even with controlled nanoscale defects, droplets spread following the Tanner law:

This result shows that nanoscale roughness‚Äîat least at the length scales tested here‚Äîdoesn't fundamentally alter the spreading mechanism. The classical theory remains robust.

Secondary Finding

The precursor film‚Äîa thin layer of liquid that spreads ahead of the main droplet‚Äîfollows a diffusion law:

This behavior had been predicted theoretically but not systematically measured on surfaces with controlled nanoscale texture. The data confirms the diffusive nature of the precursor film even in the presence of surface defects.

Scientific Significance

What This Adds to the Field

This work provides systematic experimental validation that classical wetting theory‚Äîspecifically the Tanner law‚Äîremains applicable on surfaces with controlled nanoscale roughness. Previous work lacked this combination of precise control over nanoscale features and statistical analysis across multiple measurements.

Surfaces with micrometric roughness show hemiwicking behavior, and atomically smooth surfaces have validated precursor films. The gap: surfaces with defects in the 20-80 nm range‚Äîwhere defect height matches film thickness. The central question remained experimentally unaddressed: does a precursor film form when surface roughness is comparable to the film itself?

Technical Contributions

The main methodological contribution was developing a reliable fabrication route for surfaces with tunable nanoscale defects (20-80 nm height, controlled density) combined with automated optical analysis. This approach enabled statistical analysis across hundreds of spreading events rather than relying on single measurements.

The multi-technique validation (AFM for topography, SAXS/SANS for nanoparticle characterization, optical microscopy for dynamics) ensured the surface properties were precisely known before measuring wetting behavior.

Potential Applications

Understanding how nanoscale surface texture affects spreading dynamics has implications for:

  • Coating technologies where controlled spreading is critical
  • Microfluidic device design with textured channels
  • Inkjet printing and nanoprinting precision
  • Oil recovery in porous geological formations
  • Agricultural pesticide efficiency on leaf surfaces

Open Questions

While this work validates classical wetting theory on nano-textured surfaces, several questions remain:

  • How do these results extend to larger defects (>100 nm) or different defect geometries?
  • Where does Tanner's law break down? The tested range (20-80 nm height, 0-12 defects/¬µm¬≤) all followed the same scaling. Larger defects or different geometries might show different behavior.
  • What happens with more complex liquids (polymer solutions, suspensions) on these surfaces?
  • Why do very small droplets (V < 0.001 ¬µL) disappear completely while the film keeps spreading? The observation is clear, but the threshold volume and the precise mechanism need quantification.
  • Some surfaces showed unexpected batch-to-batch variations in oxide layer formation. This affected film visibility on the 1190¬∞C surfaces but not spreading dynamics. Why?

Future work could systematically vary defect geometry while maintaining height and density, or explore how chemical heterogeneity (defects with different surface chemistry) affects the spreading behavior.
