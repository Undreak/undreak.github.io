Wetting Dynamics on Nano-Textured Surfaces

A systematic study of how liquids spread on precisely engineered surfaces at the nanoscale

The Big Picture

When you spill water on a table, it spreads. Simple, right? But what happens when that surface isn't smooth‚Äîwhen it has tiny bumps and valleys just nanometers tall? This is crucial for everything from waterproof coatings to lab-on-a-chip devices.

Despite decades of research, no one had systematically studied how liquid droplets spread on surfaces with controlled nanoscale roughness. This project aimed to answer: Do the classical laws of wetting still apply when surfaces have nanometer-scale defects?

The Challenge

Traditional wetting theory assumes perfectly smooth surfaces. But real-world surfaces are never perfectly smooth‚Äîthey have defects, roughness, and texture. The question was:

Can we control nano-scale surface features precisely enough to study their effect on wetting dynamics?

The technical challenges:

  • Creating surfaces with controlled nanoscale defects (20-80 nm tall)
  • Measuring liquid spreading at micrometer scales in real-time
  • Analyzing hundreds of droplets to get statistically meaningful results

My Approach

1. Engineering Nano-Textured Surfaces

I fabricated 32 different surfaces using silica nanoparticles. By controlling the sintering temperature (1130-1190¬∞C) and incubation time (0-50 minutes), I could precisely tune:

  • Defect height: 20-80 nanometers (about 1/1000th the width of a human hair)
  • Defect density: 0-12 defects per square micrometer

2. Characterization with Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM)

I characterized over 45 surfaces using AFM, creating 3D maps of the nanoscale topography. This wasn't just about measuring roughness‚Äîit was about understanding exactly how the surface structure affects spreading.

Technical specs:

  • Scan area: 10√ó10 Œºm¬≤
  • Resolution: <5 nm
  • Data extracted: defect height, density, and 3D profiles

3. Automated Analysis Pipeline

Here's where it got interesting. I had to analyze >300 wetting sequences‚Äîeach one showing how a droplet spreads over time. Doing this manually would take months.

I built a Python pipeline that:

  • Automatically detects interference patterns in the wetting film
  • Extracts droplet radius and film width over time
  • Fits the data to physical laws

Tools: NumPy, SciPy, scikit-image

4. Multi-Scale Characterization

To validate the surface fabrication, I measured nanoparticle diameter using two complementary techniques:

  • SAXS (Small-Angle X-ray Scattering) at XEUSS facility
  • SANS (Small-Angle Neutron Scattering) at ILL

Result: 106.2 ¬± 8.5 nm diameter (exactly as expected!)
