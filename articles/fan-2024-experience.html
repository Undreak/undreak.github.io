Three days of neutron scattering training at Institut Laue-Langevin

Personal Journal June 25-27, 2024 Grenoble, France

In June 2024, I attended the Formation Annuelle √† la Neutronique (FAN) organized by 2FDN and ILL in Grenoble. As the only master's student accepted that year, I got three days of theory, hands-on instrument time, and access to one of Europe's leading neutron research facilities‚Äîan opportunity I didn't want to waste.

Everyone else was either doing a PhD or already working as a permanent researcher. I followed the soft matter & biophysics track, which focused on SANS and spectroscopy techniques.

Arrival in Grenoble for the FAN 2024 training

Day 1: Introduction and Theory

The morning started with an introduction to neutronics. We covered neutron production methods (reactors and spallation sources), basic neutron properties, and why neutrons are useful for condensed matter research. The main advantage is that neutrons interact with atomic nuclei rather than electron clouds, which means they can distinguish between isotopes‚Äîparticularly hydrogen and deuterium. This makes them valuable for soft matter studies where you can use isotopic substitution to highlight specific components.

After the lecture, we toured the ILL facility. The scale was overwhelming. Dozens of instruments spread across multiple buildings, each designed for specific measurements‚Äîdiffractometers, spectrometers, small-angle scattering instruments. The reactor itself sits at the center, sending neutrons through guides to all these different beamlines.

The Institut Laue-Langevin neutron research facility in Grenoble

The afternoon split into two tracks. I went to the soft matter sessions. Fabrice Cousin covered SANS theory‚Äîhow you scatter neutrons at small angles to measure particle sizes and shapes. The math was straightforward enough: scattering intensity as a function of momentum transfer gives you structural information via Fourier transforms.

The next session covered spectroscopy techniques (time-of-flight and backscattering). This was harder to follow. The room was hot‚Äîno AC in summer‚Äîand the derivations moved quickly. But the main idea stuck though: measure the energy change of scattered neutrons to see molecular dynamics.

We had dinner together that evening. Talking to the other participants helped‚ÄîPhD students and permanent researchers, but everyone there to learn something outside their usual expertise. The experience gap mattered less than I'd expected.

Day 2: SANS Practical

The second day was the SANS practical on the SAM instrument. Nicolas Martin and Fabrice Cousin supervised. I brought silica nanoparticles from my thesis work‚Äîthe same ones I'd used to make nano-textured surfaces at CEA Saclay. The manufacturer specified 100 nm diameter, but I'd never verified it directly.

The SAM (Small Angle Multi-detector) instrument at ILL

Sample holder ready for SANS measurement

The measurement of my sample took minutes. For this, you load the sample, set the detector distance, configure the measurement time and position, and start the acquisition. The scattering pattern showed up on screen within minutes, confirming we'd configured it correctly and the data acquisition was going smoothly.

We configured the instrument to measure the samples of everyone, to automatically position the beam at the right sample for the right time and let it run for the night.

The instrument in operation during data acquisition

Day 3: Data Analysis

Thursday morning was for data analysis and modeling. We worked through the SANS data processing steps in more detail‚Äîhandling background scattering, fitting different structural models, extracting size distributions. Watching Fabrice analyze the samples was informative. Different systems (polymer micelles, bilayers) required different approaches, which showed the flexibility of the technique.

Raw SANS scattering data from silica nanoparticles

We fit the data with a sphere model. Result: 106 nm ¬± 8 nm. The manufacturer's spec was 100 nm, so about 6% difference‚Äîwell within normal variation. It validated what I'd been assuming, which was useful even if not strictly necessary for the thesis.

I had a train to Paris that afternoon, so we only had time to cover the basics‚Äîbackground subtraction, model fitting, size distribution extraction. Not ideal, but it showed the full workflow from measurement to result.

Spherical model fit to experimental SANS data showing particle size determination

Takeaways

The training covered the complete workflow: theory, measurement, and analysis. Seeing them connected on a working instrument clarified how the technique actually functions in practice.

I now have a better sense of when SANS is appropriate for a problem and when it's not. The spectroscopy section was harder to absorb in the short time frame, but enough to understand what the technique measures and how to read papers that use it.

Being the youngest participant mattered less than expected. Everyone was there to learn something outside their usual work. Three days isn't long, but it showed me what working in this field actually looks like and that I want to be part of it.
